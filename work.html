Heritage Craft Brand · Artware · Japan

Elmes of Japan | Union
The Origin of ARTWARE

A brand film for Union, exploring the concept of "ARTWARE" — objects that exist at the intersection of functional craft and visual art. Rooted in the Elmes of Japan philosophy, the film traces the origin of this idea through material, gesture, and the hands of makers. Shot to honor the weight and texture of physical objects in a world increasingly defined by digital surfaces. A meditation on why handmade things still matter.

Launching TX
Motion Data Factory

A launch film for Telexistence's Motion Data Factory — a platform that captures, refines, and deploys human motion data to train next-generation robots. Heritage Sake Brand · Kobe / Nada · Since 1659

Kikumasamune
菊正宗

Founded in 1659, Kikumasamune is one of Japan's oldest and most revered sake breweries. This brand film needed to make the invisible visible: the weight of 360 years of craft, the silence of cedar barrels in a Nada storehouse, the moment tradition becomes taste. Shot with vintage lenses to match the depth and grain of the subject, the film speaks to both Japanese heritage audiences and international luxury buyers discovering the world of honjozo.

Director's Statement

"I am not interested in the difference between old and new.
I am interested in what survives the translation —
the thing that remains true when the medium changes."

Japan trained me to see slowly. The country has a word — ma — for the charged silence between things. The pause before a brush meets paper. The moment a craftsman's hand stops moving and simply waits. I learned cinematography inside that tradition, and it shaped every instinct I have about when to cut and when to hold.

But I also live in a world where a product launch happens in 48 hours, where the viewer is deciding in the first three seconds, where the visual language of technology moves faster than any editorial trend. I had to learn to work in that register too — and I found that the same principles apply. Precision. Economy. The courage to let a single image carry the weight.

What I bring to international brands is not a "Japanese aesthetic" as a decorative style. It is a method of attention — the ability to find what is essential in a subject, strip everything else away, and let the camera bear witness rather than perform. That method works in a barrel house in Gifu. It works in a data center in Seoul. And it works in a pitch room in San Francisco.
